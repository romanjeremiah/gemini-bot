**Current NICE position (NG222, updated 2022, with 2024 clarifications):**

**On withdrawal severity**
- Withdrawal can be "severe and prolonged" in some patients — NICE explicitly moved away from the old "mild and self-limiting" framing.
- Symptoms typically start within 5 days of dose reduction, can include anxiety, insomnia, low mood, dizziness, electric shock sensations, nausea.

**On tapering**
- No fixed-rate taper. NICE recommends "hyperbolic" or individualized tapering — smaller reductions as the dose gets lower.
- For sertraline specifically: the drug's half-life means symptoms may emerge days after a cut, not immediately.

**On rate**
- NICE does not endorse the old "halve every 2 weeks" approach.

**What your GP should be doing**
- Discussing withdrawal risk *before* starting the taper
- Offering dose flexibility (liquid formulation, smaller tablet strengths, or compounding for the final stages)
- Planning review points — NICE suggests more frequent check-ins during reduction
- Distinguishing withdrawal symptoms from relapse (onset pattern helps: withdrawal hits faster, often with somatic symptoms; relapse is slower, mood-first)

The current NICE guidance (specifically NG222) emphasizes a hyper-individualized approach to tapering. They move away from fixed schedules in favor of a "slow and gradual" method tailored to the person's specific experience.

The key principles are:

*   **Slow Tapering:** Reductions should be gradual. There is no single standard rate, but the focus is on minimizing withdrawal symptoms.
*   **Hyperbolic Tapering:** While not explicitly naming the mathematical curve, the guidance supports the concept that reductions should get progressively smaller as the dose decreases, because the impact on serotonin receptors becomes more significant at lower doses.

**Kimi K2.6** — _7987ms · 296 out / 3924 in (3840 cached)_

Technical register activated.

**Durable Objects**: Right tool for this job. Co-location with your Worker, strong consistency, built-in hibernation that doesn't bill you for idle time. The single-threaded execution model is actually a feature for per-chat state — no concurrency headaches. Cold starts can sting (~100-200ms), but for AI bots with streaming responses, that's usually buried in generation latency.

**Upstash Redis**: Fast, familiar, runs over HTTP so it works anywhere. But you're paying for another network hop on *every* turn of a chat, and unless you're careful, you're serialising/deserialising your entire session state each time. The "serverless Redis" pricing looks cheap until your bot gets conversational and you realise it's a chatty protocol doing round-trips for field updates.

**Honest take**: If you're already on Workers, DOs are the obvious choice. The "lock in" argument is weak here — it's per-chat ephemeral state, not your crown jewels. Redis only makes sense if you need cross-platform portability or you're doing heavy set operations across sessions (leaderboards, etc.) that DOs would handle poorly.

Bad practice I'm sassy about: using Redis for this because "it's what I know" and then implementing optimistic locking in Lua because you hit race conditions. Just use the abstraction that gives you correctness by default.
